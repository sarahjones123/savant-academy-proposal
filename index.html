Good documentation doesn't just explain a product — it gives people the confidence to use it without second-guessing themselves.

10+ years of technical writing taught me one thing above everything else: content that nobody reads has already failed. The words might be perfect, the structure might be clean — but if a real person sat down, got confused, and quietly gave up, the content didn't do its job.

That's what I kept thinking about while putting this proposal together. Not "what features does Savant have?" but "what does an analyst actually need to know before Monday's close?" or "what's the one thing that makes an accountant trust a number they didn't produce themselves?" Every track in this proposal started from a question like that — not from a feature list.

I've built learning hubs from scratch using tools like MapleLMS, so I know what the first week of content production actually feels like — and what quietly falls apart at scale if you don't plan for it early. This isn't just a structure. It's a set of decisions I'm confident enough to put my name on.

Section 01

Who v1 is for

Based on what I know about Savant so far, I'm assuming there are at least three types of users who would interact with the Academy differently. These are working assumptions — the actual audience breakdown would need to be validated with the team before content production begins.

📊
Priority 1
Analyst

The person doing the actual work — connecting sources, cleaning data, building pipelines, producing outputs. When analysts feel capable, they become the product's biggest internal advocates.

Why first: Analysts have the deepest relationship with the product. When they hit a wall, they leave — or they pull the whole team with them. When they succeed, they tell everyone. That's where I'd put the most energy in v1.
⚙️
Priority 2
Admin / IT

Sets up the workspace, connects the data sources, manages who has access to what. Nobody thinks about the admin until something breaks — but a confused admin quietly blocks the entire team from getting started.

Why second: One admin unblocking a team of ten is a better use of content effort than ten individual help articles. I'd keep this track tight and practical — the goal is getting them to "everything is connected and working" as fast as possible.
🧾
Priority 3
Accountant

Reviews reports, validates numbers, signs off on outputs. Their feature surface is narrower than an analyst's, but their anxiety about accuracy is higher. They need to trust what they're looking at before they'll use it.

Why third: Not because accountants matter less — but because their learning path is genuinely shorter. A focused 4-module track covers what they need in v1. Going deeper is a v2 decision, made with real usage data.
🏷️

Every course is tagged by role, so learners see immediately what's relevant to them. Nobody should have to read a course description and wonder "is this for me?" — the tag answers that before they even click.

Section 05

What's not in v1

These areas are valuable for the long-term Academy vision, but introducing them in v1 would increase complexity, duplicate existing ecosystems, or create maintenance overhead before the foundational learning experience is fully established.

✕
Fragmented enablement experiences

Savant already has documentation, a knowledge base, templates, a video library, and community support. The Academy's job in v1 is to connect these into a structured learning flow — not add another standalone destination that learners have to find and navigate separately.

✕
Community forum integration

Savant already has a great community built on Circle.so. The Academy should link to it, not try to replicate it. Creating a second place for community discussion splits the conversation and means maintaining two spaces instead of one.

✕
Highly customised learning paths

Account-specific paths mean writing and maintaining variations of the same content for different customers. That multiplies the workload quickly. v1 should focus on reusable modules that work for most learners. Personalisation can come later, once we know what patterns actually emerge from usage.

✕
Certification programs

Certifications only mean something when the learning paths behind them are stable and the assessments have been properly validated. Rushing into certifications before that foundation exists turns them into a badge for completing content — not proof that someone actually knows how to use the product.

💬

None of these are permanently off the table. They're just not the right first move. Each one becomes a real conversation after v1 establishes a stable foundation and we have actual usage patterns to build on.

Section 04

How I'd measure success

From a technical writing perspective, the core question is simple: did people understand the content, and did it help them do something? These three areas tell me that.

✍️
Clarity
  • Module completion rate — if people drop off mid-module, the content lost them
  • Quiz pass rate per module — below 70% usually means the explanation wasn't clear enough
  • How often learners re-watch a video or retake a quiz — a signal that something didn't land the first time
  • Any module with high drop-off gets reviewed and rewritten, not just flagged
👍
Usefulness
  • Post-module rating — a simple 1–5 star or thumbs up/down after each module
  • Optional "what was missing?" field to catch gaps the rating alone won't show
  • Certification exam results — consistent wrong answers on the same question point to a content gap, not a learner gap
  • Any module rated below 3 stars on average gets looked at within the same sprint
🗂️
Reach
  • Are the right people finding the right tracks? Role-tag filter usage shows whether the structure is working
  • Which tracks does each user type actually complete — versus start and abandon
  • Are analysts ending up in admin content, or accountants in analyst tracks? That's a navigation or labelling problem
  • Number of certifications issued per role, per month
🔁
Iteration
  • A regular review cycle — monthly in the first quarter, quarterly after that
  • Every piece of feedback goes somewhere: a logged issue, a rewrite, or a note explaining why no change was made
  • Content that performed well informs what gets built next
  • The goal isn't a perfect launch — it's a system that gets better with use
Section 02

How I'd build it

Fourteen weeks from start to full v1 launch. The sequence is designed so something useful goes live early, and each phase uses what we learn from the one before.

Weeks 1–4
Foundation
  • Platform setup (ex: MapleLMS)
  • Track 1 — Foundations
  • Track 3 — Admin Essentials
  • Role tagging system
Weeks 5–9
Core tracks
  • Track 2 — Analyst Core
  • Track 4 — Accountant
  • Walnut demo scripts for all tracks
  • Quiz and assessment writing
  • Content review with target users
Weeks 10–14
Advanced + iteration
  • Track 5 — Analyst Advanced
  • First content revision cycle
  • Soft launch to pilot accounts
  • Feedback reviewed and logged
  • Broad rollout
Post-launch
v2 signals
  • Developer track (if signal)
  • Content gap analysis
  • Community integration
  • Advanced module planning
Context

What exists today vs. what this builds toward

The existing Savant Bootcamp on Circle.so shows the team already cares about education. The Academy isn't a replacement — it's what the bootcamp grows into once it has structure, sequencing, and a way to know whether it's working.

Current Bootcamp (Circle.so)
  • ·Community-hosted, discussion-board format
  • ·Good hands-on lessons, but no clear sequence
  • ·No role-based filtering — everything in one feed
  • ·No certification or formal completion tracking
  • ·No way to measure whether it's actually working
  • ·No defined path from beginner to confident user
Analytics Academy v1
  • ·Structured LMS with role-specific tracks
  • ·Every module: video + Walnut demo + quiz
  • ·Role tags on every course so learners self-select
  • ·Certification exams and shareable badges
  • ·Completion and engagement analytics from day one
  • ·A clear path from "just signed up" to "Savant Expert"
